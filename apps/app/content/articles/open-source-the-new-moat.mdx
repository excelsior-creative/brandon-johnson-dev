Here's a thing I've come to believe and it's going to annoy some recruiters: in an AI-augmented world, the résumé is worth a lot less and the public repo is worth a lot more.

Not because résumés are dead. They're not. But because the work of making a polished résumé — the framing, the metrics, the alignment — is now a two-hour LLM task. Every applicant's résumé looks the same kind of good. The signal is gone.

What hasn't been commoditized: what you actually build in public. That's my thesis. The moat for individual builders in 2026 is visible proof-of-work.

## The signal problem

Walk through the stack of things an employer used to use to assess you:

- **Résumé.** Now: LLM-polished. Signal: near zero.
- **Cover letter.** Now: LLM-generated, tailored per job. Signal: near zero.
- **Take-home project.** Now: LLM-assisted in ways that are undetectable at submission. Signal: compromised.
- **Live coding interview.** Now: still real, but expensive to run and easy to game with prep.
- **Public repos with your name on real commits.** Signal: unchanged. Possibly stronger.

The one thing that hasn't degraded is the thing that's hardest to fake: an active git history on a repo that has actual users. You either did the work or you didn't. You either understand the codebase you're claiming to have contributed to or your issues sit unmerged for six months.

## What counts as proof-of-work

This is the part where people roll their eyes, because "open source" has been career advice since 2008. But the bar has shifted. "I have a repo with 3 stars" no longer counts. "I contributed a lockfile update" no longer counts. The new bar is higher and it rewards depth, not breadth.

What actually counts in 2026:

- **Merged code in a framework with real users.** Not your demo of a framework; contributions to somebody else's framework. The social skill of navigating a maintainer's standards and shipping through their review is doing work on top of the code itself.
- **A library that solved a real problem someone else had.** Stars follow problem-solving, not naming.
- **Tools you can demonstrate in five minutes.** The "I built this, here's the two-minute Loom" loop is devastatingly effective.
- **Public postmortems.** If you shipped it and it broke, the write-up is career capital.

The common thread: it has to be auditable. Someone can open the repo and see the quality of the work in thirty seconds.

## Why this is accelerating

Three forces are pushing the career economy toward proof-of-work:

1. **LLMs make polished deliverables cheap.** When everyone can produce a great-looking artifact, artifact quality stops being a selection signal. Depth and durability are the signals that survive.

2. **The hiring market is saturated at the top of the funnel.** Employers drown in résumés. They're looking for sorting mechanisms that don't cost them time. "Send me a repo" is free and self-filtering.

3. **Teams want contributors who can work asynchronously in public.** Remote work plus AI-augmented output means the baseline is shifting toward builders who can ship, document, and defend their work without a manager standing over them. Open source is exactly that environment at zero cost to the hiring team.

## My own honest accounting

I'm a contributor to Eliza. I'm a contributor to OpenClaw. Those aren't résumé decorations. They are the things that get me hired into rooms that closed-source work would not get me into, because every one of those contributions is auditable. Someone can read my PRs. Someone can see how I handle code review. Someone can see how I write tests and how I write commit messages and how I respond when a maintainer asks me to rework something.

None of that is visible in a closed-source job at a private company, no matter how impressive the work was. The closed-source work still matters — it pays the bills, it taught me the craft — but it doesn't travel. Public commits travel forever.

## What to actually do about it

Concrete, this week:

- Pick one OSS project you use regularly and contribute something real. Not a typo fix; a feature they had in their issues list, or a bug you hit. Budget a weekend.
- Start a public repo that solves the problem you had last week and expected someone else to have solved. Write the README like you're explaining it to a stranger, because you are.
- Write up a thing you shipped — at work, in private — without exposing proprietary details. The framing and the lessons are what matters, not the specifics.
- Resist the temptation to start ten things. One deep contribution outweighs ten shallow ones.

## The counterargument

The pushback I get on this: "I work at a company that doesn't let me open-source anything." Fair. The answer is not "quit." The answer is that the boundary between your work and your public work has to be small enough that your public work shows your craft, even if it's evening-and-weekend time.

I know engineers who maintain serious OSS contributions alongside demanding day jobs. I know engineers who don't. The first group has options the second group doesn't. That's the moat.

## The one-line summary

The résumé is now a filter everyone can pass. The public repo is the thing only the actual builders can produce. In 2026 and forward, the moat is the repo.

Go build in public.
